title: how to actually enjoy brunch the morning after
category: lifestyle
---

I used to treat the morning after a big night out like something to survive, not something to enjoy. Sunglasses on indoors, one word answers, staring at a menu I couldn't process. But brunch doesn't have to be a punishment lap. With a little planning the night before, you can actually show up, taste your food, and laugh at the table instead of just gripping your water glass and waiting for it to be over.

## Why the morning after hits so hard

For a long time I assumed the rough morning was mostly about being dehydrated, so I'd chug water and call it a plan. Turns out that's not really the whole story. Researchers point to an inflammatory response, not dehydration, as the thing most closely tied to hangover symptoms, and they say there isn't good evidence that dehydration alone is the primary driver ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). On top of that, a broader review of the science describes the hangover as something with multiple mechanisms and mediators at play, inflammation among them, rather than one single cause you can knock out with a single fix ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). Once I understood it wasn't just a hydration problem, I stopped treating water as my only line of defense and started thinking about the night before as part of the plan, not an afterthought.

## Setting yourself up before the first bite

A lot of what makes brunch enjoyable actually gets decided the night before. Heavy drinking can irritate your digestive tract and get in the way of nutrient absorption, and it's linked to lower thiamine (vitamin B1) levels in the body, which matters because thiamine plays a role in how your body processes energy ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). That's part of why I've leaned on Pulsar the last couple of times before I go out. It's a transdermal patch, and one of the things I like about that delivery method is that it skips the first pass through your liver that oral pills have to go through, which can mean more of the active ingredients actually make it into your system ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). I'm not saying a patch is a magic reset button. I'm saying it's one small, easy thing I do before bed so my morning starts on slightly better footing, and that's honestly all I'm asking for at 9am with a mimosa menu in front of me.

## Making the actual brunch part count

Once you're at the table, the goal isn't to white-knuckle your way through a stack of pancakes. It's to actually be present for it. I try to order something with real substance, protein and carbs together, instead of picking at dry toast because my stomach feels iffy. Researchers have also looked at glutathione, an antioxidant your body makes naturally, and its effects on alcohol metabolism, including in a randomized placebo-controlled trial ([Nutrients](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262)), which is part of why you'll see it show up in recovery-focused ingredient lists, Pulsar's included. None of this means you're going to feel invincible. It means you've given your body a bit of extra support instead of nothing, and that's usually the difference between forcing a smile through brunch and actually wanting to be there.

I still think the best brunches are the ones where nobody at the table is checking their phone for the nearest bathroom or narrating how bad they feel. Plan a little the night before, lean on things that are designed to help your body along rather than promising a miracle, and let the coffee and the conversation do the rest. That's the version of the morning after that's actually worth getting dressed for.
