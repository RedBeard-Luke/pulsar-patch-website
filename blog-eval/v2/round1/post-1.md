title: Why We Put It On Your Skin And Not In Your Stomach
category: science
---

I get asked this a lot: why did we build Pulsar as a patch instead of just another pill or powder you chug before bed? The short answer is your gut is already dealing with enough after a night of drinking, and we wanted a route that skips it entirely.

## Your Stomach Is Not In Great Shape Right Now

When you drink heavily, your digestive tract takes a hit. Heavy alcohol use can inflame the digestive tract and block the absorption of vitamins, and it's linked to thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency in particular ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). That means the same system you'd normally rely on to absorb a morning-after supplement is running below capacity right when you need it most. Swallowing a pill and hoping your irritated gut does its job well feels backwards to me. I wanted a way to get ingredients moving without asking a stressed-out stomach to do the work.

## How A Patch Actually Gets Ingredients In

This is where the skin comes in. Transdermal patches deliver actives through the skin and avoid the first-pass metabolism that oral pills go through, which can improve how much of the active reaches the bloodstream ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). In plain terms, a pill has to survive your stomach acid, get processed by your liver, and lose some of itself along the way before it ever does anything. A patch sidesteps that whole obstacle course. You stick it on, and the ingredients absorb through the skin directly into circulation instead of waiting in line behind digestion. For us, that's the whole point of building Pulsar this way, not as a gimmick but as a more direct path when your gut already has its hands full.

## What We're Actually Trying To Support

I want to be upfront that a patch is not a cure for a hangover, and honestly nothing is, because a hangover isn't one single thing you can just switch off. Researchers describe it as being driven by multiple mechanisms and mediators, including inflammation, rather than any single cause ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). Some of that same research points toward an inflammatory response as a bigger piece of the puzzle than people assume, with one review noting hangover symptoms are linked to an inflammatory (cytokine) response rather than dehydration, which it says lacks good evidence as the primary cause ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). That's part of why we didn't design Pulsar around just "drink more water" logic. We wanted ingredients that are relevant to what's actually happening in your body, delivered in a way that doesn't depend on a gut that's already struggling.

One ingredient we get questions about is glutathione, which has been studied for its effects on alcohol metabolism and hangover in a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial ([Nutrients/MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262)). We're not going to tell you it erases anything. What we can say is that it's a compound worth taking seriously, and putting it on a patch is our way of trying to get it into circulation efficiently instead of routing it through a digestive system that's not exactly firing on all cylinders after a big night.

## Why This Matters The Morning After

At the end of the day, Pulsar is designed to support your body through the rough parts of the morning after, not to promise you'll wake up like nothing happened. The patch format is simply the delivery method we believe gives ingredients the best shot at doing their job, by going through skin instead of a stomach that's already overworked. You stick it on before bed or in the morning, and it works in the background while you go about getting your day started, rather than sitting in your gut waiting to be broken down.

If you've ever wondered why we didn't just make a pill, that's the reasoning. It's not about being different for the sake of it. It's about picking the route that makes the most sense when your body is already dealing with a lot, and letting the science of transdermal delivery do what oral routes can't.
