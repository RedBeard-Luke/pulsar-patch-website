I used to think a hangover was just "dehydration plus regret." Turns out the real story going on inside you is a lot more interesting, and a lot more useful to understand if you actually want to feel more like yourself the next day. Here's what's really happening under the hood, and why we built Pulsar around it.

## It's Not Just About Water

For years the go-to explanation for hangovers was dehydration. Drink more water, problem solved, right? Not quite. Researchers now point to an inflammatory response, meaning your immune system releases cytokines that drive a lot of the misery, and there isn't good evidence that dehydration alone is the main culprit ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). That lines up with a broader review of the science, which describes the hangover as something driven by multiple mechanisms and mediators working together, including that same inflammatory piece, rather than any single cause you can pin it all on ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). So the pounding head and full-body "why did I do this" feeling isn't one switch flipping. It's several systems getting knocked off balance at once.

## Your Gut And Vitamin Levels Take A Hit Too

While your immune system is busy overreacting, your digestive tract is dealing with its own damage. Heavy drinking can inflame the digestive tract and interfere with how your body absorbs vitamins, and it's specifically linked to a drop in thiamine, also known as vitamin B1 ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). Vitamin B1 matters for how your body converts food into usable energy, so it's not hard to connect the dots between a rough night out and that "running on empty" feeling the next morning. This is part of why we don't think of a hangover as a single symptom to knock out. It's your gut, your immune system, and your nutrient levels all sending distress signals at the same time.

## The Antioxidant Angle

Your body also leans on its own internal antioxidant systems while it's processing alcohol, and one that keeps coming up in research is glutathione. Scientists have specifically studied glutathione's role in alcohol metabolism and hangover symptoms through a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, which is about as rigorous as clinical research gets ([Nutrients, MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262)). That kind of study is exactly why we pay attention to which ingredients we put in a Pulsar patch and why. We're not chasing trends, we're following what's actually been tested.

## Why Delivery Method Matters

None of this matters much if what you're taking doesn't actually get where it needs to go. Oral supplements have to survive digestion and pass through the liver before they reach your bloodstream, a process called first-pass metabolism. Transdermal patches, on the other hand, deliver actives through the skin and skip that first pass, which can improve how much of the active ingredient actually makes it into circulation ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). That's the whole reason Pulsar is a patch and not another pill. When your stomach is already unhappy the morning after, asking it to break down and absorb a capsule feels like a losing bet. A patch is designed to work with your body instead of asking your gut to do more work while it's already struggling.

Put it all together and a hangover isn't one problem, it's several overlapping ones: inflammation, nutrient disruption, and your body's own antioxidant defenses all working overtime. Pulsar is built to support your body through that whole tangle, not to promise it away. Understanding what's actually happening under the surface is the first step to helping your body handle the morning after with a little more grace.
