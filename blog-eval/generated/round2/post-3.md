I used to think a hangover was just "too much wine catching up with me," full stop. But once I started digging into the actual science behind it, I realized there's a lot more going on under the hood than a simple punishment for a fun night. If you're going to deal with the morning after, it helps to actually understand what your body is doing to you.

## What's Actually Happening In Your Body

At the most basic level, hangovers are driven by dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, and symptoms tend to peak right around the time your blood alcohol level returns to zero ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). That's part of why the worst of it usually hits in the morning, once the alcohol itself is basically gone but the aftermath is just getting started. Your body isn't fighting the alcohol anymore at that point, it's dealing with the mess left behind, and that mess shows up as the headache, the nausea, the fatigue, all of it.

This is one of the reasons I got interested in Pulsar in the first place. Instead of asking you to choke down a pill on a stomach that already feels off, it's a transdermal patch, and transdermal delivery works by sending actives through the skin instead of routing them through the gut and liver the way an oral pill has to ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). When your digestive system is already having a rough morning, skipping that step just makes sense to me.

## Why Your B Vitamins Take A Hit

Something people don't talk about enough is what alcohol does to your nutrient levels overnight. Drinking depletes thiamine, which is vitamin B1, and it can also get in the way of your body properly absorbing and using what thiamine you do have ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). Thiamine plays a role in how your body processes energy, so when it's running low, it's not hard to connect the dots to why you feel so drained and foggy the next day. This isn't about one glass of wine wrecking your vitamin stores forever, it's more that a night of drinking puts a real dent in resources your body was counting on.

That's part of why Pulsar is built around ingredients meant to support your body through that depletion rather than promising to erase it. Nobody on our team would ever claim a patch cures a hangover, because a hangover isn't a disease with a cure, it's your body working through what you put it through the night before. What we're going for is helping you feel a bit more like yourself while your body does that work.

## Why Some Mornings Are Worse Than Others

If you've ever wondered why one night out leaves you fine and another leaves you flattened, you're not imagining it. Hangover severity actually varies quite a bit between people, and it's associated with how much alcohol you consumed ([Nature, Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0)). So it's not purely about willpower or "handling your liquor," there's a real, measurable relationship between the amount you drink and how rough the next day gets, on top of whatever your own body's baseline tolerance happens to be.

Knowing that has genuinely changed how I think about a night out. It's not about dreading the morning after every time, it's about being realistic that more drinks generally means a tougher recovery, and planning accordingly. That might mean pacing yourself, it might mean reaching for water between rounds, or it might mean giving your body a little extra support before you even go to bed. Understanding the why behind hangovers is what makes it possible to actually prepare for them instead of just white-knuckling through the next morning and hoping for the best.
