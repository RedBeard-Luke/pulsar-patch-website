---
title: How I Made It Through a Full Wedding Weekend Without Falling Apart
category: lifestyle
---

Weddings are never just one night anymore. You've got the welcome drinks on Thursday, the rehearsal dinner Friday, the ceremony and reception Saturday, and then somehow a "casual brunch" on Sunday where everyone pretends they feel great. I said yes to being a bridesmaid this spring and quickly realized surviving a three-day wedding is a completely different sport than surviving one big night out.

## The math of a multi-day open bar

The thing nobody warns you about is that a wedding weekend isn't one hangover, it's a slow accumulation. A glass of champagne at the welcome party turns into wine at dinner, which turns into shots on the dance floor, and you're doing some version of that three nights in a row. Hangover severity isn't the same for everyone and it tracks pretty closely with how much you actually drink, which checks out with how much worse Saturday felt compared to Thursday ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0)). By the time the reception rolled around I wasn't starting from zero, I was starting from "already a little behind."

I stopped trying to match everyone drink for drink and started pacing myself around the events I actually cared about, like the toasts and the first dance, instead of the pregame. That alone made the weekend feel less like damage control.

## Why the mornings hit so hard

Part of why wedding mornings feel brutal is pretty straightforward once you look at what's actually happening in your body. Hangovers are largely driven by dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, and symptoms tend to peak as your blood alcohol comes back down to zero, which for me was usually right around the time my alarm went off for hair and makeup ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). Add in a few hours of actual sleep because the reception ran late, and it's no surprise the bridal party group chat was mostly just people sending the vomit emoji at 8am.

Alcohol also depletes thiamine, which is one of those B vitamins your body leans on, and heavy drinking can make it harder to absorb and use properly, which is probably part of why I felt so foggy by day two ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). Knowing that helped me stop blaming myself for being "weak" and just plan around it instead.

## What actually got me through it

I built a little routine and stuck with it every single morning of the weekend. Ginger tea became my best friend, since ginger has actually been studied for supporting digestion and easing nausea, and my stomach genuinely felt calmer after a cup ([Nutrients, MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262)). I also leaned on Pulsar every morning before I even got out of bed. Pulsar is a transdermal patch, which means it delivers through your skin instead of your stomach, and that route skips the first-pass breakdown your gut and liver put oral pills through ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). For someone whose stomach was already not thrilled with three days of champagne, not having to choke down a pill was honestly a relief.

I'm not going to pretend Pulsar made me forget the weekend happened. It's not a cure, it's just support, and I treated it that way. I paired the patch with water, actual food at every event, and letting myself nap between the ceremony and the reception instead of powering through on caffeine.

## The real lesson from the weekend

Multi-day events reward planning way more than willpower. I wasn't the person who felt best because I drank the least, I was the person who felt best because I stopped treating every night like it was the only one that mattered. By Sunday brunch I was tired, sure, but I was upright, smiling in photos, and actually able to eat my eggs instead of just staring at them. For a wedding weekend, that's basically winning.
