I get asked about NAC and glutathione more than almost any other ingredient question, so I wanted to actually break down what these two are doing once they're in your system, not just repeat the buzzwords you see on every label. They're related, they work together, and once you understand the relationship, the rest of the label makes a lot more sense.

## The NAC to glutathione pipeline

NAC stands for N-acetylcysteine, and on its own it's not the antioxidant doing the heavy lifting. Instead, NAC is a precursor to glutathione, which is one of your body's key antioxidants involved in detoxification processes ([National Library of Medicine](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). Think of NAC as the raw material your body needs on hand so it can build more glutathione when it needs it. That's why you'll see NAC listed as an ingredient instead of glutathione itself in a lot of formulations, it's about giving your body the building block rather than trying to deliver the finished antioxidant directly.

## Why a rough night puts extra demand on this system

A night of drinking puts your body through a lot at once. Alcohol is known to deplete thiamine, or vitamin B1, and can get in the way of your body absorbing and using it properly ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)), and that depletion is part of a bigger pattern of your system working overtime to process what you drank. Separately, the rough morning-after feeling most of us know is tied to dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, with symptoms tending to peak as your blood alcohol level comes back down to zero ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). None of that is Pulsar making a claim about what alcohol does to you, it's just the backdrop that explains why people started looking at NAC and glutathione in the first place: your body already has a detox system, and a big night is exactly the kind of thing that leans on it.

## Why we didn't put NAC in a pill

Once we knew we wanted NAC in the Pulsar patch, the delivery method mattered as much as the ingredient. Oral supplements have to survive your stomach and get processed by your liver before anything reaches your bloodstream, and that first pass through your gut and liver is exactly what a transdermal patch is built to skip, since patches deliver actives through the skin instead ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). That's the whole reason Pulsar is a patch and not another capsule sitting in your cabinet. We wanted a way to get NAC to your system that didn't depend on your stomach cooperating, especially on a morning when your stomach is the last thing you want to rely on.

## What this means for how you use Pulsar

I'm not going to tell you NAC and glutathione are some magic switch, because that's not how any of this works, and it's not how we talk about our own product either. What I will say is that understanding the actual mechanism, precursor in, antioxidant support out, makes it a lot easier to see why we chose the ingredients we did. Pulsar is designed to support your body's own systems during the parts of the morning after that are genuinely rough, not to override them. If you're the type who wants to know what's actually in your patch and why, hopefully this gives you a real answer instead of a marketing line.
