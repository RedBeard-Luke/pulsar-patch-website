title: How I Survived a Full Wedding Weekend Without Losing a Day

Weddings are never just one night anymore. It's a Thursday rehearsal dinner, a Friday welcome party, the ceremony and reception on Saturday, and then a farewell brunch on Sunday that somehow still has a mimosa tower. I love a good wedding weekend, but I learned the hard way that you can't treat it like one big party and expect to walk away in one piece.

## The Weekend Adds Up Faster Than You Think

The thing nobody tells you about a multi-day wedding is that it's not four separate nights, it's one long stretch where your body barely gets a chance to reset in between. Hangover severity tracks pretty closely with how much you actually drink, so a toast at the rehearsal dinner plus champagne at the ceremony plus an open bar at the reception is a very different math problem than a single night out [Nature (Scientific Reports)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0). By the time Sunday brunch rolls around, a lot of guests are running on fumes and mimosas, which is exactly how you end up napping through the group photo.

## What Is Actually Happening To Your Body

A hangover isn't just "feeling bad," it's a real physiological pileup of dehydration, inflammation, and wrecked sleep, and it tends to hit hardest right around the time your blood alcohol level gets back to zero, which for a wedding weekend can mean waking up at 6am for the brunch shuttle feeling like a truck hit you [Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers). On top of that, drinking steadily depletes thiamine, one of the B vitamins your body relies on, so the more nights in a row you're drinking, the more your reserves get chipped away [Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/). That's part of why day three of a wedding weekend feels so much rougher than day one, even if you technically drank less.

## How I Actually Made It To The Farewell Brunch

I stopped trying to just power through on willpower and started being a little more strategic about it. I put on a Pulsar patch before the rehearsal dinner and again before the reception, since it's designed to work through your skin instead of your stomach, and transdermal delivery skips the first-pass breakdown your liver and gut put oral pills through, so it's already working while everyone else is still fumbling for a bottle of water and some aspirin [Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route). I also leaned on ginger tea between events, since ginger has actually been studied for supporting digestion and easing that queasy morning-after feeling, and it doubles as a decent excuse to step outside the reception for a few quiet minutes [Nutrients (MDPI)](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262).

## A Few Ground Rules For Next Time

I'm not saying skip the champagne toast or bail on the after-party, that's half the fun of a wedding weekend. But pacing yourself across four days instead of treating every night like the last night out makes a real difference. Water between drinks, actual food before the open bar opens, and giving your body some support instead of just hoping for the best all add up. Pulsar isn't magic and it's not going to erase a weekend of celebrating, but it's designed to help your body handle the ups and downs of a long stretch of toasts a little better, so you're not the one person asleep at brunch while everyone else is still telling stories about the best man's speech.

By the time I hugged the happy couple goodbye on Sunday, I actually remembered doing it, and I was upright for the group photo. That's a real win when you've got four days of a wedding behind you.
