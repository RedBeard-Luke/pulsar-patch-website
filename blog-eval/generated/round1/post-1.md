I get this question a lot: if a patch is just sitting on your skin, how does anything actually get into your bloodstream? It feels like it shouldn't work, but skin is not the sealed wall we think it is. It's more like a series of doors, and the right formula can knock on them the right way.

## Why Your Gut Isn't Always The Best Route

When you swallow a pill or a capsule, it doesn't go straight to work. It has to survive your stomach acid, get absorbed through your intestinal lining, then pass through your liver before it ever reaches the rest of your body. That last step is called first-pass metabolism, and it's a big deal because your liver can break down or filter out a chunk of whatever you just took before it circulates. Transdermal patches skip that whole chain. They deliver actives straight through the skin and into the bloodstream, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract and the liver's first pass entirely, according to [Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route). That's the whole premise behind Pulsar. We're not asking your stomach to do anything when it's already dealing with a rough morning.

## What's Actually Happening At The Skin Level

Skin looks solid, but it's built in layers, and the outermost one, the stratum corneum, is the real gatekeeper. It's tough, but it's not impenetrable. Small, well-designed molecules can move through it and into the layers underneath, where capillaries pick them up and carry them into circulation. This is why the actual patch matters so much. It's not enough to just tape a vitamin to your arm. The formulation has to be built so ingredients can actually cross that barrier at a usable rate over a few hours instead of dumping everything at once or not moving at all. That's the engineering problem transdermal delivery has to solve, and it's why we spent so much time on the adhesive and ingredient blend instead of just copying a pill and slapping it on skin.

## Why This Matters The Morning After

A rough night out isn't just one thing going wrong, it's dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep all showing up at once, and symptoms tend to peak as your body finishes processing the alcohol, per [Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers). On top of that, how rough you feel isn't universal. Research has found that how bad a hangover hits varies a lot person to person and tracks with how much you drank the night before, per [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0). Your gut is usually the last thing that wants to cooperate on a morning like that. Asking it to break down a pill when it's already unhappy is a big ask. Routing support through the skin instead means Pulsar doesn't need your stomach to be in a good mood to do its thing.

## The Bigger Picture

None of this is about being flashy for the sake of it. It's a workaround for a real limitation in how your body processes what you swallow. Oral supplements have to survive a gauntlet before they're useful, and a lot gets lost along the way. Skin delivery is a different path entirely, one that sidesteps that gauntlet. That's the whole bet we made with Pulsar: build something that supports your body's morning-after recovery from a direction your gut isn't blocking.
