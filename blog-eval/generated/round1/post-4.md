---
title: What Alcohol Actually Does To Your B Vitamins
category: science
---

I used to think a hangover was just "dehydration plus regret," but there's a whole vitamin story happening in the background that most people never hear about. Every time you drink, your body is quietly burning through B vitamins faster than usual, and that has more to do with how rough the next morning feels than most of us realize. I wanted to break down what's actually going on, because once you see it, the way you think about recovery starts to shift.

## Why alcohol drains your B vitamins in the first place

Your liver treats alcohol like an intruder that needs to be processed and removed, and that metabolic cleanup job is expensive. It uses up nutrients, including B vitamins, at a higher rate than normal. Thiamine, also known as B1, takes a particularly hard hit. Alcohol consumption depletes thiamine and can actually impair your body's ability to absorb and use the thiamine you do have, which is a double whammy since you're losing it faster while also getting worse at holding onto it ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). On top of that, drinking tends to come with worse eating habits and more trips to the bathroom, both of which mean less B vitamin intake and more loss through urine. It's not one single mechanism, it's a handful of small leaks that add up over a night out.

## The B1 connection nobody talks about at brunch

Thiamine deficiency doesn't get nearly the attention it deserves in casual conversations about drinking, but it's one of the more well documented nutritional effects of alcohol. Your nervous system and energy metabolism both lean on B1 to function properly, so when levels drop, people can feel foggy, low energy, or just generally "off" in a way that's hard to pin on any one thing. This is separate from the classic hangover symptoms most of us associate with a rough night. Those symptoms, the headache, the nausea, the wiped-out feeling, are their own separate mess tied to dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, and they tend to peak right around when your blood alcohol level hits zero ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). B vitamin depletion is more of a slow burn on top of all that, a quieter drain that can stick around a bit longer than the acute hangover itself.

## Why the amount you drink matters more than you think

Here's the part that surprised me most: this isn't a one-size-fits-all effect. Research shows hangover severity actually varies quite a bit between individuals and tracks closely with how much alcohol was consumed ([Nature, Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0)). So a bigger night out isn't just "more of the same" the next day, it's a genuinely different physiological load, and that includes more B vitamin depletion to go along with it. This is part of why two people can have the same number of drinks and feel completely different the next morning. Genetics, food intake, sleep, and hydration all play a role, but the sheer volume consumed is a real variable your body has to deal with.
