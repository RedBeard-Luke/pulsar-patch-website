I get asked about NAC and glutathione more than almost any other ingredient we talk about, and honestly I get it, the names sound like they belong in a lab, not on a night out. So let's break down what these two things are actually doing in your body, no jargon, no hype.

## The Basics: NAC Is The Precursor, Glutathione Is The Payoff

Glutathione is one of the body's main antioxidants. Your cells make it on their own, and it plays a central role in helping your body manage everyday oxidative stress and support its natural detoxification processes. The catch is that glutathione itself is hard to supplement directly because it breaks down before it can do much good. That's where NAC comes in. N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is a precursor to glutathione, meaning your body uses it as a building block to make more of the real thing ([National Library of Medicine (PMC)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). So when you take NAC, you're not adding antioxidant directly, you're giving your body the raw material to make its own.

I think this distinction matters because it's easy to assume NAC and glutathione do the exact same job. They don't. NAC is upstream. Glutathione is downstream. One feeds the other.

## Why Delivery Method Actually Changes The Story

Here's a piece people don't think about enough: how you get an ingredient into your body changes how much of it actually shows up where it needs to be. Oral supplements have to survive your stomach acid and then get filtered through your liver before anything reaches your bloodstream, a process called first-pass metabolism. Transdermal delivery skips that step entirely, sending actives through the skin straight into circulation instead of routing through the gut and liver first ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). That's a big part of why we built Pulsar as a patch instead of another pill. We wanted a format that gets ingredients like NAC working with your body's own processes without asking your stomach to do the heavy lifting first thing in the morning, when it's already not thrilled with you.

I'm not saying a patch is magic. I'm saying delivery method is a real variable, and it's one we thought hard about.

## Why This Matters The Morning After

A rough night out puts real stress on your system. Hangovers themselves are driven by a mix of dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, and the worst of it tends to hit right as your blood alcohol level comes back down to zero ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). Your body is already juggling a lot in that window. This is exactly the kind of moment where having support for your natural antioxidant processes makes intuitive sense, since your system is working overtime regardless.

To be clear, NAC and glutathione aren't a fix and we're not claiming they treat or prevent anything. What we can say is that they're two of the most well-studied compounds in the antioxidant and detox-support conversation, and understanding what they actually do (instead of just seeing the names on a label) helps you make an informed choice about what you put on your skin or in your body.
