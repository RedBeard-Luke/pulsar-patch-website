---
title: How To Actually Enjoy Brunch The Morning After
category: lifestyle
---

I used to treat the morning after a big night out like something to survive, not enjoy. I'd sit at brunch squinting at the menu, order dry toast out of fear, and spend the whole meal waiting to feel human again. Somewhere along the way I decided that was backwards. Brunch after a night out should be one of the best meals of the week, not a punishment lap. Here's how I actually get there.

## Set Yourself Up Before You Even Sit Down

The truth is brunch enjoyment starts the night before. A lot of what makes you feel rough the next morning comes down to dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep, and those symptoms tend to peak right around the time your blood alcohol hits zero, which for most people is mid-morning ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). So before I even think about a table reservation, I'm drinking water between rounds and again before bed. It's not glamorous, but it changes the whole trajectory of the next day. I also throw on a Pulsar patch before I fall asleep, since I'd rather support my body overnight than scramble for a fix once I'm already sitting across from a stack of pancakes feeling like garbage.

## Order Like You Mean It

This is where I used to sabotage myself. I'd panic-order the blandest thing on the menu because I assumed my stomach couldn't handle anything else. But there's real reason to lean into a few specific foods instead of avoiding flavor altogether. Ginger shows up a lot in brunch dishes for good reason. It's been studied for its anti-nausea and digestive-support properties, which makes a ginger-spiced baked good or a ginger tea a smarter order than plain crackers ever were ([Nutrients, MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/19/3262)). I also make a point to get eggs, whole grains, or leafy greens on the plate, since alcohol depletes thiamine (vitamin B1) and can get in the way of your body absorbing and using it properly, and those foods are solid sources ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). Basically, eat the interesting thing, not the safe thing.

## Stack Your Recovery Tools So You're Not Relying On Willpower

Nobody enjoys brunch while white-knuckling through a headache, so I don't leave recovery to chance. Pills are fine, but they have to survive your stomach and get processed by your liver before anything happens, and if your gut is already unhappy from the night before, that route can feel slow or unreliable. That's part of why I like a patch instead. Transdermal delivery gets actives through the skin and skips first-pass digestion and liver processing entirely, which is a pretty different mechanism than swallowing something and hoping ([Pharmacy Times](https://www.pharmacytimes.com/view/delivery-methods-the-patch-versus-the-oral-route)). I put mine on before bed so by the time I'm at brunch, I'm not thinking about it at all. I'm just eating.

## Give Yourself Permission To Actually Sit There

The last piece is honestly mental. Brunch is supposed to be slow. It's supposed to involve a second coffee, a little too much conversation, and maybe an order of fries you didn't plan on. If you spend the whole meal bracing for how bad you might feel, you miss the actual point of the tradition, which is sitting with people you like and eating something good. Plan ahead, order what sounds good instead of what feels "safe," and let the rest take care of itself. That's the whole trick.
