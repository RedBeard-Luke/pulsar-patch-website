category: science

I used to think a hangover was just "too much alcohol, not enough water." Turns out it's way more layered than that, and once I actually dug into the science, I understood why my usual fixes never worked as well as I wanted. Here's what's really going on in your body the morning after, broken down without the jargon.

## Your Body Is Fighting Three Battles At Once

A hangover isn't one single thing. It's the combined effect of dehydration, inflammation, and wrecked sleep, and symptoms tend to peak right as your blood alcohol level drops back to zero, which is exactly when you feel the worst ([Cedars-Sinai](https://www.cedars-sinai.org/stories-and-insights/healthy-living/science-of-hangovers)). That's why you can feel awful even after the alcohol itself has technically left your system. Your body isn't reacting to the alcohol anymore, it's dealing with the aftermath: fluid loss, an immune response that ramps up inflammation, and sleep that was lighter and more fragmented than you realized.

It also explains why two people can drink the same amount and end up in completely different shape the next day. Research shows hangover severity varies a lot between individuals and tracks with how much alcohol was actually consumed, but it's not a perfectly predictable curve ([Nature, Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92676-0)). Some of that comes down to hydration status going in, how much you slept, and what your body had to work with nutritionally before the night even started.

## The Vitamin Most People Never Think About

One piece that surprised me: alcohol depletes thiamine, also known as vitamin B1, and can get in the way of your body absorbing and using it properly ([Alcohol and Drug Foundation](https://adf.org.au/insights/alcohol-related-thiamine-deficiency/)). Thiamine is involved in how your body converts food into usable energy, so when it's running low, it makes sense that you feel drained, foggy, and generally off the next day. This is one of those quiet mechanisms that doesn't get talked about as much as dehydration, but it's a real piece of the puzzle.

## Why Your Body's Cleanup Crew Gets Overwhelmed

Your body has its own detox pathways, and one of the key players is glutathione, a major antioxidant. N-acetylcysteine, or NAC, is a precursor your body uses to help produce glutathione and support those detoxification processes ([National Library of Medicine, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6761819/)). Processing alcohol puts a real load on that system, which is part of why your body feels like it's playing catch-up the next morning. None of this means glutathione support "cures" a hangover, but it does show why giving your body the raw materials it needs matters.

It's not one villain, it's dehydration, inflammation, disrupted sleep, and nutrient depletion all showing up together. Once you see the whole picture, it's a lot easier to plan ahead instead of just hoping tomorrow-you gets lucky.
